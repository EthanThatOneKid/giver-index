### Chart 1 — Score Distribution & Data Coverage
![Score Distribution](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/EthanThatOneKid/giver-index/ad14763/docs/visualizations/01_distribution.png)

**What it shows:** Two panels. Left: a histogram of GIVER scores across all 199 scored countries, with the global mean (43.3) and the circular/linear threshold (50) marked. Right: a pie chart showing how many countries have strong data coverage (≥2 independent signals), partial coverage (1 signal), or no score at all.

**Why it matters:** The distribution is right-skewed — most countries cluster between 30–50, meaning the world is more linear than circular by this index's current measure. Only 3 countries exceed 60. The coverage pie reveals that over 40 countries have no data signal yet (shown gray on maps).

### Chart 2 — Regional Comparison
![Regional Comparison](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/EthanThatOneKid/giver-index/ad14763/docs/visualizations/02_regional.png)

**What it shows:** Two panels. Left: bar chart of mean GIVER score by region, sorted from highest to lowest. Right: box plot showing the full score spread within each region — not just the average, but how much countries within the same region vary from each other.

**Why it matters:** Europe leads but hides enormous internal variation. Asia-Pacific has the widest spread: Japan and South Korea score 61+, while several Central Asian nations score below 20. The box plot confirms that "regional averages" are almost meaningless — the real story is the within-region range.

### Chart 3 — Dimension Radar: Top 5 vs Bottom 5 Countries
![Dimension Radar](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/EthanThatOneKid/giver-index/ad14763/docs/visualizations/03_radar.png)

**What it shows:** Two radar (spider) charts, one for the top 5 most circular countries and one for the bottom 5 most linear. Each country's shape reflects their score across all five GIVER dimensions: Generative (G), Impact (I), Verifiable (V), Evidence-based (E), and Rooted (R). Scores range from 0–100 per dimension.

**Why it matters:** No country dominates all five dimensions — and that is the point. Different societies lead on different dimensions, confirming the index captures real multidimensional cultural variation rather than a single general "development" factor. The bottom 5 countries show nearly flat, low shapes — their linear archetype is consistent across all dimensions.

---

### Chart 4 — Data Signal Coverage Heatmap
![Coverage Heatmap](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/EthanThatOneKid/giver-index/ad14763/docs/visualizations/04_heatmap.png)

**What it shows:** A heatmap where rows are the five GIVER dimensions and columns are six world regions. Each cell shows what percentage of countries in that region have an available data signal for that dimension (darker green = more countries have the data). Cells annotated with percentages.

**Why it matters:** This reveals where the index is data-rich and where it is thin. Impact (GDP per capita) is covered for nearly 100% of countries globally — that dimension is reliable. Evidence-based (WVS self-expression) is well-covered for Europe and Americas but sparse for Africa and Asia. Verifiable (GitHub + patents) is sparse everywhere — this is the dimension that most needs the GitHub Archive pipeline (#6) to be built.

### Chart 5 — Top 20 vs Bottom 20 Countries
![Top 20 vs Bottom 20](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/EthanThatOneKid/giver-index/ad14763/docs/visualizations/05_top_bottom.png)

**What it shows:** Side-by-side horizontal bar charts. Left: the 20 countries with the highest GIVER scores, sorted descending. Right: the 20 countries with the lowest scores. Color-coded: blue = strong circular (≥60), teal = circular borderline (50–59), yellow = moderate linear (35–49), orange = linear (20–34), red = extreme linear (<20). The threshold of 50 is marked as a vertical line in both charts.

**Why it matters:** The top 20 countries are tightly clustered between 50–72 — only Luxembourg (72.2) and Taiwan (63.7) score notably above the rest. The bottom 20 are all below 22, forming a long tail. The clustering in the middle zone (35–55) reveals that most of humanity occupies the transition zone between linear and circular — not firmly in either camp, which is exactly what the GIVER Displacement Theory predicts.
